# Pruvost Lexical Borrowing — Arabic → French / Arabic → Romance

**A COMPILATION of documented etymologies, NOT a discovery or novel claim.**

**Source:** Jean Pruvost, *Nos Ancêtres les Arabes : Ce que notre langue leur doit* (279 pp.)
**Dataset:** `pruvost-borrowings.csv` (78 entries)
**Date compiled:** 2026-06-12
**Status:** All entries flagged for human verification of source references.

---

## Headline Statistic

**Arabic is the third-largest source of loanwords in French**, after English and Italian (Pruvost pp. 29, 54, states this repeatedly across the book, citing the relative chronological depth of Italian and Arabic borrowings vs the more recent English influx). A secondary quote from Salah Guemriche (*Dictionnaire des mots français d'origine arabe*, 2007) is cited on p. 4:

> "Il y a deux fois plus de mots français d'origine arabe que de mots français d'origine gauloise ! Peut-être même trois fois plus…"

Between **500 and 600 Arabic-origin words** are in standard French dictionaries (this figure reflects the commonly cited estimate; Pruvost's book surveys several hundred across 18 thematic chapters).

## What Is Documented

### 1. Route Classification

Pruvost identifies **six routes** (pp. 57–64) by which Arabic words entered French:

| Route | Description | Example |
|-------|-------------|---------|
| **Direct** | Crusades/Mediterranean trade | coton, café, sucre, magasin |
| **Via Spain** | Andalusian transmission | algèbre, alcade, aubergine, abricot |
| **Via Italy** | Italian city-state mediation | artichaut, caraque, risque (disputed) |
| **Via Latin** | Scholastic/medical Latin intermediary | alchimie, alambic, chiffre, zéro |
| **Via English** | English as intermediate borrower | safari, mohair (1860) |
| **Colonial** | French colonial contact in North Africa | toubib, bled, clebs, wech |

Only ~30% of words entered via the "direct" route; the majority passed through a second language.

### 2. The "al-" Fossilization Pattern

A large subset of Arabic→French borrowings preserve the Arabic definite article **al-** at the word's onset (often simplified to a- before consonants or fused into the root). This is the single most recognizable morphological marker of Arabic origin in French. Documented set from Pruvost:

- Scientific/mathematical: **algèbre** (al-jabr), **alchimie** (al-kīmiyā), **alambic** (al-anbīq), **alcali** (al-qālī), **almanach** (al-manākh)
- Dyes/chemicals: **alcool** (al-kuḥl → kohl → alcohol), **alkermès** (al-qirmiz → crimson), **alizarine** (al-uṣāra), **alquifoux** (al-kuhl)
- Administrative: **alcade** (al-qāḍī → alcade/cadi), **algésiras** (al-jazīra), **algarade** (al-ghāra)
- Plants: **abricot** (al-barqūq → via Spanish), **aubergine** (al-bādhijān → via Catalan), **artichaut** (al-kharshūf → via Italian)
- Astronomical: **almicantarat** (al-muqanṭarāt), **alidade**

Many of these words entered Romance languages via Andalusia and the Spanish *el-* article (cognate to Arabic *al-*), so the article fossilization *predates* entry into French. The pattern is diagnostic of Arabic-origin borrowings in Spanish and Portuguese with even higher frequency.

### 4. Semantic Domains

Pruvost's chapters are organized by semantic domain, showing that Arabic borrowings touch nearly every area of French life:

- **Food & cuisine** (~20 words): café, sucre, abricot, artichaut, aubergine, épinard, estragon, caroube, cumin, couscous, kebab, sirop, sorbet
- **Textiles & clothing** (~15 words): coton, jupe, gilet, gabardine, satin, mousseline, gaze, moire, mohair, babouche, saroual
- **Science & mathematics** (~15 words): algèbre, algorithme, chiffre, zéro, alchimie, alambic, alcali, azimut, zénith, nadir, borax, camphre
- **Trade & administration** (~10 words): magasin, douane, tarif, amiral, cadi, alcade, douane, avarie
- **Military** (~10 words): algarade, razzia, alfange, caraque, sarbacane, arsenal
- **Colloquial / argot** (~10 words): bled, clebs, toubib, seum, wech, kif, baraka, flouze

## What This Supports (without needing the three-roots claim)

Arabic lexical influence on French is **established language-contact scholarship**, not a novel claim. Pruvost's book does not argue for deep genetic cognation, nor for a unified "root" system (ATUM/BULL/TOR). It is a straightforward documentation of borrowing patterns during the medieval and early modern periods — precisely the kind of evidence the ATUM project needs as **contextual support** without requiring the controversial three-roots framework.

**Specific ways this supports the broader thesis:**
- Arabic lexical richness contributed measurably to European languages; this is not in dispute.
- The "al-" article fossilization demonstrates a structural trace of Arabic in Romance that is recognizable even to non-specialists.
- The 500–600 documented borrowings establish Arabic as a significant substrate source for French vocabulary.

**What it does NOT support:**
- Not evidence of deep genetic relationship (Arabic and French are from different language families — Afro-Asiatic and Indo-European).
- Not evidence of the ATUM/BULL/TOR root system (this dataset is purely lexical-borrowing, not root-etymology).
- Not evidence of universal linguistic patterns (documented language contact is language-specific by nature).

## Unsourced / Disputed Entries

Two entries flagged for human follow-up:

1. **risque ← rizq** (Pruvost pp. 68–69): The standard French etymological dictionaries classify *risque* as a borrowing from Italian *rischio*, with ultimate origin contested. Pruvost presents a case (citing Bencheikh, CNRS) that Italian *rischio* itself derives from Arabic *rizq* used in 13th-century Toledo legal documents. This is **not the standard view** and is flagged `disputed`.
2. **échevin** (Pruvost p. 32): Possible connection to Arabic *ṣāḥib* is noted but the Germanic *skabin* etymology is better supported.
