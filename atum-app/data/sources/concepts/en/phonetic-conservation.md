# Why Did Arabic Preserve the Sounds?

---

## The Startling Comparison 🔬

| Language | Sounds Lost | Example |
|----------|-------------|---------|
| Arabic | **0** | Preserved all 28 sounds |
| Greek | −5 | Lost P, TH, KH, GH, SH gutturals |
| Latin | −12 | Lost many guttural consonants |
| English | −15+ | Heavily distorted remaining sounds |

---

## Why This Matters

Every sound a language loses = a "thread" connecting it to the original root is cut.
Arabic with 28 sounds ← widest connection network with ancient roots.

**Concrete example:**
- Guttural ح (strong H) exists in Arabic 🔬
- Lost in Greek, Latin, and English
- Therefore: "hayyizi" (enclosure) → "Isis" connection is only visible through Arabic

---

## Ibdal Rules — The Bridge Between Languages 🔬

Al-Qubaysi documented **75 phonetic substitution rules** between phonetically adjacent letters.
- **68%** of these rules are between letters from adjacent articulation points 🔬
- Example: ب ↔ م ↔ و (labials) | ك ↔ ق ↔ غ (velars)

These rules explain how an Arabic word became Greek, then Latin, then English.

---

## Practical Result

When you find an unknown word in any language:
1. Search for its root using ibdal substitution rules 🔬
2. Compare with Arabic roots from adjacent articulation points 🔬
3. Focus on the root meaning, not the literal meaning 🧩

Arabic is not the "origin" of every language — but it is the **closest living language to the shared ancient root**. 🧩
